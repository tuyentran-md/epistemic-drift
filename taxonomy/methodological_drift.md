# Type 4: Methodological Drift

## Definition

AI suggests or modifies analytical approaches in ways that optimize for "interesting results" rather than methodological rigor — a form of automated, invisible p-hacking.

## Mechanism

1. Researcher runs initial analysis — results are modest or non-significant
2. AI is consulted: "How can I analyze this differently?" or "What other approaches could work?"
3. AI suggests subgroup analyses, alternative statistical tests, variable transformations, or different model specifications
4. One of these produces a significant or interesting result
5. The paper presents this analysis as the planned approach

## Why it's different from traditional p-hacking

Traditional p-hacking requires the researcher to:
- Know multiple analytical approaches
- Try them manually
- Consciously select the one that works

AI-assisted methodological drift:
- Generates dozens of approaches instantly
- Frames each suggestion as methodologically justified
- Provides citations supporting the chosen method
- The researcher may genuinely believe they found the "right" approach

## The expertise asymmetry

Methodological drift is most dangerous for researchers who are:
- Early career (less statistical training)
- Working outside their methodological comfort zone
- Under pressure to produce significant results
- Using AI specifically because they need analytical help

These are exactly the researchers most likely to trust AI's methodological suggestions without critical evaluation.

## Detection signals

- Unusual or overly complex analytical approaches for the study design
- Multiple sensitivity analyses that all happen to confirm the main finding
- Statistical methods that seem sophisticated but unnecessary for the research question
- Methods section that reads like a textbook explanation rather than a practical description

## Countermeasures

1. **Pre-register analytical plan** — Lock methods before seeing results
2. **Statistical review by a methodologist** — Independent check of analytical choices
3. **Report all analyses** — Including AI-suggested approaches that didn't "work"
4. **Justify method choices independently of results** — "We used method X because..." should not include "...because it showed significant results"
